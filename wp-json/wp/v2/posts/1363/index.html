How did free Blacks use legislative petitions to protest racial oppression in antebellum Delaware?<\/h3>\r\n

By: Krishanna Prince | Posted: 7-7-2022<\/p>\r\n

Before the Fifteenth Amendment extended voting rights to Black men, free Black Delawareans were unable to use the ballot to challenge oppressive laws or elect leaders who would advocate for their liberation.[1]<\/sup> However, through petitioning, Black voices enter the historical record for us to examine. Petitioning\u00a0was common in antebellum America for historically oppressed groups\u00a0who were denied participation in formal politics.[2]<\/sup>\u00a0As documents, petitions transform human emotions of pain and outrage into textual grievances directed towards those in power such as legislatures and courts.[3]<\/sup>\u00a0In Delaware, free Blacks petitioned against\u00a0\u201c\u2026nuisance laws fashioned to support racial social control\u201d which came to be notoriously known as \u201cBlack codes.\u201d[4]<\/sup><\/p>\r\n

For my research, I looked to Black-authored sources by examining petitions filed by free Blacks to Delaware\u2019s legislature between 1830 and 1869. These petitions come from the University of North Carolina at Greensboro (UNCG)\u2019s\u00a0Race & Slavery Petitions Project\u00a0<\/em>which collected\u00a0<\/em>2,975 legislative petitions and 14,512 county court petitions made by enslaved people, free people of color, and whites in slaveholding states between 1775 and 1867.[5]<\/sup> Collections of these petitions are physically located in two series of reels in the University of Delaware\u2019s microform room in Morris Library.[6]<\/sup> This research project examines twelve legislative petitions to investigate how free Blacks in Delaware framed their grievances as racial oppression in response to four laws that comprised the \u201cBlack Codes.\u201d The voices of free Black petitioners shed light on how they used petitions to protest such laws passed by the Delaware legislature. Legislative petitions were used by free Blacks to detail their lived experiences of Black Codes as criminalizing their activity and restricting their freedom, emphasize their good character, expose the contradictory nature of Black codes, and appeal to the interests of legislators.<\/p>\r\n

Analyzing the Petitions:\u00a0Arguments for Alleviation of Oppression<\/em><\/h4>\r\n

Free Blacks used legislative petitions to protest several oppressive laws passed by the Delaware legislature in the antebellum period.\u00a0The Act of 1832 prohibited free Blacks from owning firearms and limited their right to practice religion. The Act of 1849 required free Blacks to present a pass or certificate certifying freedom to pass through or travel from the state, which had to be signed by a white man. The Acts of 1851 prohibited free Blacks from traveling out of state for more than sixty days, visiting Delaware if they were non-residents, and practicing religion. The act also allowed white people to take free Black children away from their families to use their labor.[7]<\/sup> Free Blacks who allegedly violated these laws were at risk of arrest, fines, incarceration, and enslavement.<\/p>\r\n\r\n
